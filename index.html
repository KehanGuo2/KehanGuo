I am a Ph.D. candidate in Computer Science at the University of Notre Dame, advised by Prof. Xiangliang Zhang. I work on RL post-training for agentic systems, asking one question in two settings: when we optimize a model against a proxy — a reward model, a verifier, a frozen environment — where does the optimization pressure actually go?

On the generative side, Reward Transport (under review) shows that the noise–data coupling in flow matching is a free interface for property alignment. On the agentic side, I study how verifiers, tools, and corpora shape — and are exploited by — the policies trained against them. Scientific reasoning is my testbed: I created ChemLLMBench (NeurIPS 2023) and MolPuzzle (NeurIPS 2024, Spotlight), both community standards.

I also collaborate broadly within my lab on RL optimization (CEPO), adaptive inference-time reasoning (AdaReasoner, NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight), and agent tooling (ChemOrch, NeurIPS 2025).

This summer I am a research intern at Apple, working on reinforcement-learning post-training for large language models.

Research

Reward and coupling as interfaces. Alignment signal can enter a training system through overlooked interfaces. Reward Transport repurposes the OT coupling in flow matching for zero-cost property control, and characterizes exactly when this interface exists (under review).

The environment side of agentic RL. Agentic RL replaces the reward function with a reward system — verifiers, tools, attribution schemes, a frozen world. I study which designs hold up under optimization pressure, where pressure relocates when one gap closes, and how policies trained on a frozen corpus decay as the live world drifts.

Scientific agents and rigorous evaluation. Benchmarks and live evaluation harnesses — ChemLLMBench, MolPuzzle, and ChemElucid-Live (under review) — that measure how models acquire, verify, and act on information: the apparatus the first two threads run on. MolPuzzle's headline finding: GPT-4o scores 1.4% on expert structure-elucidation tasks.
